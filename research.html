Protoplanetary Disc Evolution

My research focuses on understanding the survival and dissipation of protoplanetary discs-the rotating environments of gas and dust where planets are born. By combining large-scale cluster surveys with multi-wavelength time-series data, I investigate the underlying physical mechanisms that allow certain rare stellar systems to preserve their discs for more than tens of millions of years.

FOCUS 01

Mapping Disc Survival

While most discs dissipate within 10 million years, my work involves conducting comprehensive blind surveys of young stellar clusters in the solar neighborhood. By tracking these populations across extended timeframes (up to 100 Myr), I identify and map the final evolutionary stages of long-lived discs that defy standard dissipation timelines.

FOCUS 02

Dynamic Variability in Older Discs

Using mid-infrared data from NEOWISE, I track how the structural profiles of these old disc systems change over time. Investigating this dynamic variability provides critical clues about active processes operating in the disc, such as shifting structural warps or the presence of newly formed, embedded planetesimals moving through the dust.

FOCUS 03

3-D Modelling of long lived "Peter Pan" discs

Using MCFOST, I model these rare discs to get insight about their three-dimensional structure which might explain their longevity. I make use of optical and infrared observations to constrain the physical properties of these systems.
